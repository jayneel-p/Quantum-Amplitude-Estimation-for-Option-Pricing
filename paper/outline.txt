Paper title (working): Quantum Monte Carlo Option Pricing Under an Honest Classical Baseline

Thesis: Quantum amplitude estimation offers a genuine quadratic speedup for
option pricing, but a defensible near-term project should separate the small
circuits we can validate exactly from the large path-dependent circuits that are
still resource-heavy. We reproduce the core Stamatopoulos et al. European-call
pipeline, validate it against Black-Scholes, and add a small three-date arithmetic
Asian circuit as a finite-grid extension. Realistic daily-monitored Asian
pricing is handled classically with Monte Carlo and Kemna-Vorst variance
reduction, with quantum Asian scaling discussed honestly.

Paper structure (target ~15 pages)
===================================

1. Introduction  (~1.5 pp)
   - Option pricing as risk-neutral expectation; simulation needed for complex payoffs.
   - Classical MC: flexible but O(1/sqrt(M)) convergence [Gla03].
   - Quantum promise: amplitude estimation gives O(1/M) [RGB18, Sta20].
   - Gap in the literature: most quantum papers benchmark against vanilla MC, not
     against variance-reduced classical methods.  This overstates practical advantage.
   - Our contribution: (a) implement both vanilla and control-variate classical MC
     as baselines, (b) reproduce and validate the Stamatopoulos European-call
     quantum circuit pipeline, (c) extend the same circuit idea to a toy three-date
     arithmetic Asian option, and (d) compare resource costs honestly.

2. Financial and mathematical background  (~2 pp)
   2.1 GBM under risk-neutral measure Q
       - SDE, log-normal terminal distribution [RGB18 Sec. II].
   2.2 European call option
       - Payoff, discounted expectation, Black–Scholes closed form [BS73] as benchmark.
   2.3 Arithmetic Asian call option
       - Discrete average payoff max(A - K, 0); no closed form.
       - Geometric Asian: closed form exists because geometric mean of lognormals
         is lognormal.  This enables the Kemna–Vorst control variate [KV90].

3. Classical Monte Carlo methodology  (~2.5 pp)
   3.1 Vanilla MC for European call
       - Path simulation, payoff averaging, CLT confidence intervals.
       - Convergence: error ~ O(1/sqrt(M)), validated against BS.
   3.2 Vanilla MC for arithmetic Asian
       - Full-path simulation via GBM discretization; averaging over time steps.
   3.3 Kemna–Vorst variance reduction
       - Geometric Asian closed form derivation (adjusted drift and vol).
       - Control variate estimator:  C_arith ≈ C_mc + beta*(C_geo_exact - C_geo_mc).
       - Optimal beta from sample covariance.
       - Standard error reduction: ~36x (variance reduction ~1,300x) at N=252.
         Literature reports ~20-40x stderr reduction depending on parameters.
         Lead with stderr reduction (comparable to literature); note variance
         factor in parentheses for context.
   3.4 Implementation note: simulate each path panel once; vanilla and KV on same ω (CRN).

4. Quantum Monte Carlo framework  (~3 pp)
   4.1 The quantum pipeline: load distribution -> payoff in amplitude -> AE
       - Block diagram following [Sta20] Fig. 1, [RGB18] Sec. IV.
   4.2 State preparation (distribution loading)
       - Encoding log-normal P(S_T) into qubit amplitudes.
       - Qiskit approach: piecewise-linear rotation or log-concave loader.
   4.3 Payoff operator
       - Mapping max(S - K, 0) into amplitude of an ancilla qubit.
   4.4 Amplitude estimation
       - Canonical AE (QPE-based) [BHMT02]: O(1/M) but deep circuits.
       - MLE-based AE without QPE [Suz20, Sta20 Sec. 5]: shallower, same scaling.
       - Iterative AE as practical middle ground.
       - In the numerical section, exact statevector readout is used as the
         deterministic validation of the A operator; AE is discussed as the
         sampling procedure that would estimate the same objective probability.
   4.5 Extension to Asian options
       - Multi-register path simulation; average oracle.
       - Additional qubit/depth overhead vs European case.
       - We implement a three-date finite-grid toy circuit only, then explain why
         realistic daily monitoring is not a one-night simulator target.

5. Numerical results  (~3 pp)
   5.1 European call: classical convergence
       - MC estimates vs BS at M = 500, 2k, 8k, 32k, 128k.
       - Error vs M plot confirming O(1/sqrt(M)).
   5.2 Asian call: vanilla MC vs control-variate MC
       - Convergence comparison: stderr vs M for both estimators.
       - Show ~1,300x variance reduction (daily monitoring, N=252):
         CV at 100k paths ≈ Vanilla MC at ~132M paths.
   5.3 Quantum European call (Qiskit statevector validation)
       - Reproduce the Stamatopoulos pipeline: log-normal distribution loading
         plus payoff-ancilla encoding.
       - Exact circuit expectation vs Black-Scholes for n = 2,...,8 distribution
         qubits.
       - Circuit resource summary after transpiling to standard simulator gates
         ry, rz, x, h, cx.
   5.4 Quantum Asian toy extension
       - Three monitoring dates, two qubits per date, 64 finite GBM grid paths.
       - Exact finite-grid Asian price vs quantum statevector payoff probability.
       - Classical three-date MC result shown as continuous-model context.
       - Discuss scaling: realistic N-date Asian circuits require O(N * n)
         state qubits plus average/payoff workspace.
   5.5 The honest comparison
       - Table: to reach epsilon accuracy, how many samples/queries does each
         method need?
         * Vanilla MC:           O(1/epsilon^2)
         * Control-variate MC:   O(1/(rho * epsilon^2))  where rho < 1
         * Quantum AE:           O(1/epsilon)
         * But each quantum query costs a nontrivial state-prep/payoff circuit,
           so gate-count adjusted comparison matters.
       - Crossover discussion: quantum wins asymptotically, but the validated
         toy circuits are demonstrations of mechanism rather than practical
         advantage claims.

6. Discussion  (~1.5 pp)
   - Variance reduction narrows the gap: quantum still wins asymptotically but
     the crossover moves to higher accuracy demands.
   - Path-dependent options (Asian) are more promising for quantum because
     classical variance reduction is less effective there than for Europeans.
   - Near-term bottlenecks: distribution loading depth, error mitigation overhead,
     limited qubit counts.
   - Comparison with PDE-based quantum approach [Ren25]: different regime
     (exponential vs quadratic speedup, but different problem formulation).

7. Conclusion  (~0.5 pp)
   - Summary of findings.
   - Quantum advantage is real but overstated when measured against strawman
     classical baselines.
   - Path-dependent and high-dimensional pricing remain the strongest use case.
   - Future work: hardware runs, multi-asset options, stochastic vol [WK24].
